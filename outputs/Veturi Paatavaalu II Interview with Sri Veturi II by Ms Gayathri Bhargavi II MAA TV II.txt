Interview with Sri Veturi Sundaramurthy
By Ms. Gayathri Bhargavi | MAA TV
Programme: Veturi Paatavaalu
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[Introduction]
Sri Veturi Sundaramurthy — lyricist behind some of Telugu cinema's most celebrated songs,
including works from Geetha, Gamanani, Gathini Marchi, Shrudeya, Laila, and many others.
This interview explores the depth and meaning behind his iconic compositions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TOPIC 1: "Nadavino Dhamunathiyam" — The Song and Its Meaning

GAYATHRI: This is from a very popular film. The song "Nadavino Dhamunathiyam" is still
          widely loved. In the lyrics you wrote — "Shivuni Nayanathriya Laasivam" — what
          does that phrase mean? What does Shiva's dance have to do with it?

VETURI:   Shiva's three eyes are a symbol of Trikala — the three planes of time.
          That is why Shiva is called Sarvajnudu — the all-knowing one.

          Each eye holds a different vision:
            • The first eye sees Bhutakala — the past.
            • The second eye sees Vartamana — the present.
            • The third eye sees Mukulita — the future yet to unfold.

          Now, consider the word "Nimisham" — a single blink of an eye. For ordinary
          human beings, time is measured in blinks. But for Shiva, who is God, there are
          no blinks. He does not blink. For us, the rap of a blink is where time begins.

          "Nayanathriya Laasivam" speaks of the cosmic dance of Shiva — the Lasya.
          In that one eternal gaze across past, present, and future lies the rhythm of
          all creation. That is Shivaswarupam.

          When I wrote that line, I was told about this by a scholar. I asked him —
          "Sir, do you know what you're asking of me?" It was a line that had to carry
          the entire weight of Shiva's cosmic vision. And yet, it had to fit the song.
          That is the challenge and the joy of writing.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

TOPIC 2: "Sagara Sangamam" — Rasasiddha Kaveshwara

GAYATHRI: In the song from Sagara Sangamam, you used the phrase "Rasasiddha Kaveshwara."
          It is connected to dance. How important is it to bring that concept into a lyric?
          What is Kavi? What is Rasa? Who is a Rasasiddha?

VETURI:   Rasa is not just emotion — it is an experience that takes place within a person.
          It is musical, it is a game, it is a lived feeling. It is not something you
          can simply describe. It must be felt.

          When you watch dance on screen — on film — if the performance is true, you feel
          as though you are inside it. That is Rasa. That sense of being drawn completely
          into what you are witnessing — that is what "Rasasiddha" means. One who has
          mastered the experience of Rasa.

          A Kaveshwara — a great poet — is not just someone who strings words together.
          He is someone who can create Rasa in the listener. That is the highest standard.
